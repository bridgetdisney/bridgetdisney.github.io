Research Interests: Data science education for librarians, data curation (including metadata and dataset discovery), research assistance services, data visualization, text analysis, data mining, and digital humanities.

Research Philosophy: I approach research from a pragmatic world view, considering what works for collecting data and understanding the problem.

Dissertation Topic: Data Science Education for Librarians and Information Scientists

Research Questions

RQ1: What are the data science skills most needed by librarians and information scientists, particularly in a research setting? (Consider specific skills above and beyond the core competencies of librarianship)

RQ2: What components of the data science profession are useful to careers in library and information science?

RQ3: Which LIS/iSchool courses teach data science skills? (regular courses and special topic courses)

RQ4: How do the data related courses meet the need for data science skills garnered from RQ1? Are there gaps in the data science training needed for library and information schools?

Hypotheses

H1: There are gaps in data science education for librarians that can be identified and improved upon.

H2: There exist a set of data science skills and competencies that apply specifically to library and research settings.

Auxillary Questions

What is the difference or advantage of being an iSchool or ALA accredited LIS school? (Analysis of data science opportunities in both.)

How can information scientists and librarians increase their data science skills outside of coursework (DataCamp, Carpentries, etc)?

Should the data science emphasis for librarians be data curation or data analysis?

Which LIS/iSchool courses encourage participation in future development of data science opportunities? (looking into the future for ideas that are not yet being implemented)

What certificate programs are being offered in library and information science schools?

Would it be feasible for library and information science programs to offer a certificate in data science for research in all fields? The library and information field offers significant advantages for understanding the development of research. The data science skills would not need to reach the computer science level of topics such as programming machine learning, but instead would be geared toward those that would like to do deeper research.

Federer, L. (2018). Defining data librarianship: a survey of competencies, skills, and training. Journal of the Medical Library Association: JMLA, 106(3), 294. Implied: What competencies, skills, and training define data librarianship? Librarians who do data-related work were surveyed about their work and educational backgrounds and asked to rate the relevance of a set of data-related skills and knowledge to their work. Quantitative analysis was conducted using a variety of packages in R and RStudio, and visualizations were created using the ggplot2 package. Open-ended responses were analyzed manually. Also, cluster analysis. This article attempts to determine the role of data librarians in the context of research data services. The author reports on a survey distributed through (mostly medical) associations and twitter hashtag groups. A taxonomy of skills was rated by the respondents using a Likert scale, and qualitative comments were noted. Additionally, using cluster analysis, the author was able to divide the respondents into two groups, one being a data generalist and the other being a subject specialist. These analyses are able to provide an overall picture of the implications of the study, and the suggestion that "new curricula may be needed to enable data librarians to adequately respond to the evolving needs of research communities.”
Harris‐Pierce, R. L., & Liu, Y. Q. (2012). Is data curation education at library and information science schools in North America adequate? New Library World. Are library schools adequately addressing this growing need to educate future librarians in data curation? Qualitative content survey of data curation courses offered at Library and Information Science (LIS) schools in North America Although this is one of the oldest articles reviewed and concentrates solely on data curation, I wanted to include it for comparison. It is a self describes “qualitative content survey” that relies on website information as well as emails sent to class instructors. The results were basically descriptive with few new revelations found. However, it will be interesting to see how data related subjects in library schools have progressed in the last ten years. The authors state that no two data curation courses are alike, so perhaps there has been some improvement in defining this field with cohesive objectives.
Jiang, H., & Chen, C. (2021). Data Science Skills and Graduate Certificates: A Quantitative Text Analysis. Journal of Computer Information Systems, 1-17. (1)  What skills were needed in each of the seven job categories defined in the EDISON Data Science Framework? (2)  What data science skills are taught in graduate data science certificates in different discipline areas and are the coverage sufficient? Quantitative text analysis (word frequencies and automated text analysis) of 5000 data science job descriptions and 588 required courses. This lengthy and detailed article scrutinized data science courses offered in graduate certificates looking for skills extracted from the seven categories defined in the EDISON Data Science Professional Profiles. The author’s unique approach begin with a general (including non-data) list of skills from LinkedIn which was used for keyword extraction from two other lists, course descriptions and job descriptions. The extracted data from these last two lists were joined to make matches between them which resulted in over 6 million matched items. From there, they removed the duplicated to determine how the certificate courses were meeting the needs of each EDISON category.
Khan, H. R., & Du, Y. (2017). What is a data librarian?: A content analysis of job advertisements for data librarians in the United States academic libraries. What is a data librarian? How do we define a professional title that is now becoming more common in academic libraries? Where do data librarians fit in the academic library ecosystem? What parts of the data curation and data lifecycle are responsibilities of a data librarian in an academic library? How to prepare students to fill these new roles as data librarians? Qualitative analysis on text of 50 random data librarian job announcements found through multiple online job boards in the United States.Content analysis (word frequency counts) of each posting to identify job requirements. By examining 50 random job announcements for data librarians, the authors of this article aim to glean information about the core competencies and skills needed for this specialty. One theme that stands out is how data librarians fit into the overall academic library structure, and also that the term “data librarian” is actually a broad term that could cover metadata, research services, geospatial, or other focused area. They came to the vague conclusion that the role needs to be more defined and that the necessary skills should be included in the LIS curriculum.

Only programs that listed a set of recommended courses. Used automated content analysis of course titles and descriptions. Examining course titles and descriptions of ALA accredited LIS programs through text analysis, the author considers the gap between the data education and the skills needed by data professionals. It is useful that she grouped the academic program content accordingly: data curation, digital curation, digital humanities, data science, knowledge management, and informatics. A cross-institutional analysis of iSchools, including a cluster analysis of courses offerings; data source was the iSchool Directory. Wang, L. (2018). Twinning data science with information science in schools of library and information science. Journal of Documentation. Implied: How does data science related to information science in LIS education? Examines mission statements and nature of works in both data and information science. Uses document theory. This paper is more theoretical than practical but it is useful for understanding the difference between the two disciplines of data science and information science in the DIKW (data, information, knowledge, wisdom) context. It aptly points out that data science is of a positivist nature in that data is concrete, but that information science analyzes the bias in the data stemming from the fact that statistical samples, while representative, are actually all different. This is something that LIS can bring to the research table.

    • Overall community for American libraries. This organization also provides accreditation for library education and addresses current topics in library and information science.

  • ALISE - Association for Library and Information Science Education

    • Bills itself as the "global leader in education for the information professions."

  • ASIS&T - Association for Information Science & Technology

    • SIG-ED Special interest group focusing on education for those handling information.
    • Information and Learning Sciences SIG

  • CODATA - Committee on Data of the International Science Council

    • Promotes FAIR data training for repositories (Findable, Accessible, Interoperable, Reusable) and advances data science training through the CODATA-RDA School of Research Data Science.

  • iSchools

    • Group of schools specifically designated as information schools.

  • MLA - Medical Library Association

    • Data science has been used quite a bit in the medical field. This organization offers a data science specialization certificate that is worth looking at.

  • RDA - Research Data Alliance

    • An incredible resource for all things data with several working groups on a multitude of topics.
